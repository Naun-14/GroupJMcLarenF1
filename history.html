The Origins of Bruce McLaren Motor Racing

Bruce McLaren Motor Racing was founded in 1963 by New Zealander Bruce McLaren. Bruce was a works driver for the British Formula One team Cooper with whom he had won three Grands Prix and come second in the 1960 World Championship. Wanting to compete in the Australasian Tasman Series, Bruce approached his employers, but when team owner Charles Cooper insisted on using 1.5-litre Formula One-spec engines instead of the 2.5-litre motors permitted by the Tasman rules, Bruce decided to set up his own team with teammate Timmy Mayer using custom-built Cooper cars.

Bruce won the 1964 series, but Mayer was tragically killed in practice for the final race. This event led Bruce to partner with Teddy Mayer, who later became the largest shareholder in Bruce McLaren Motor Racing Limited.

Though based in England (Feltham and later Colnbrook and Woking), Bruce never used the typical British racing green. His first Formula One car—the McLaren M2B—raced in white and green, referencing a fictional team from the film "Grand Prix."

During this period, Bruce also raced sports cars in the UK and North America and took part in the 1965 Tasman Series with Phil Hill. In 1966, believing Cooper's performance was declining, he began racing his own team’s cars in Grands Prix.

Bruce's initiative, vision, and engineering drive laid the foundation for what would become one of the most iconic teams in Formula One history.
